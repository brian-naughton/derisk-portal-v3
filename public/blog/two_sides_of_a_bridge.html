Where It Started

DeRisk was born from a simple question: if DeFi has already lost at least $7 billion to smart contract exploits — and the true total, depending on definitions and scope, is significantly higher — at its current scale, what happens when traditional finance moves onchain?

The convergence is already underway. BlackRock is tokenising money market funds. JPMorgan is settling trades on blockchain rails. The world's largest financial institutions are building on the same smart contract infrastructure that has been exploited, repeatedly, for nine-figure sums. The attack surfaces don't shrink as the stakes grow — they compound.

And nobody is immune. In November 2025 — three weeks after Vitalik Buterin had publicly argued that bluechip DeFi had matured past its most dangerous phase — Balancer V2 was exploited for $128M through a precision loss vulnerability that had been sitting in production code for years. If a battle-tested protocol with 11 audits under its belt can still harbour exploitable flaws, the security model for digital assets is not fit for what's coming next — as TradFi and DeFi converge. The industry obsesses over Total Value Locked when it should be fretting about Total Value Lost.

We are careering towards a crisis. Point-in-time audits cannot keep pace with adversaries who probe continuously. Chain-specific tooling cannot see across the bridges where the biggest losses occur. And no existing framework translates risk into the language that institutional capital requires before it can act. This is not a tooling problem. It is an infrastructure problem — and it demands an infrastructure-grade solution.

This is that solution.

The immediate users of this infrastructure are the actors responsible for safeguarding capital onchain: exchanges deciding whether to list new assets, custodians evaluating protocol exposure, asset managers allocating across DeFi, and protocol treasuries managing billions in pooled liquidity. Each of these decisions requires a continuous, machine-readable assessment of smart contract risk — something point-in-time audits were never designed to provide.

The Rosetta Stone

Every blockchain runs on a different virtual machine — Ethereum has the EVM, Solana has the SVM — each with its own programming language, its own execution model, and its own class of vulnerabilities. Security tooling has historically been locked to one VM at a time.

We developed a canonical way of representing the archetypes of smart contract vulnerabilities such that they can be translated between any virtual machine environment. We call this patent-pending architecture Canonical Intermediate Representation (CIR) — or, colloquially, the "Rosetta Stone." It provides a form of universality across chains that has never existed before.

In practice: the DeRisk engine takes source code from any blockchain, translates it into CIR primitives through VM-specific emitters, then analyses it with a single universal rule set. Solidity on Ethereum and Rust on Solana go through different emitters but get evaluated by the same 67 vulnerability rules, the same actuarial model trained on 1,400+ historical exploits, the same scoring framework. The result is a single comparable risk score across chains, protocols, and execution environments — the kind of signal required for institutional capital to price smart contract risk rather than simply avoid it.

Part of the development process was training and calibrating the engine against DeFi's most devastating exploits. To do that properly, we needed to be certain we were analysing the smart contract code that was actually live in the lead-up to each attack — not patched versions deployed after the fact. That requirement led us to build what we now call the Time Machine: a module that lets us travel back to the moments before an exploit, retrieve the vulnerable source code, and run our scanner against exactly what the attacker saw. In traditional financial risk modelling, historical stress testing is foundational — the Time Machine performs the same role for DeFi.

Seeing Both Sides at Once

On February 2, 2022, an attacker minted 120,000 wETH on Solana and redeemed it for real ETH on Ethereum. Total loss: $320 million. The Wormhole Bridge — connecting the two largest smart contract ecosystems — was drained in a single transaction.

We pointed our engine at both sides. The Solana contract in Rust. The Ethereum contract in Solidity. Two languages, two VMs, one scan. The findings told the same story from opposite ends:

  • Solana (entry point, score: 100/100) — Guardian validation is bypassable. Validator quorum sits at the minimum threshold. Message processing is vulnerable to manipulation. Four distinct findings, plus a bridge cluster multiplier confirming they compound.
  • Ethereum (exit point, score: 94/100) — Signature verification will accept forged guardian approvals. The contract will honour the fraudulent message and release real assets.
Forged on Solana. Redeemed on Ethereum. That's the $320M exploit — and the engine flags both ends.

But training against history only proves you can see the past. The real question is whether the rules generalise.

The Smoking Gun

In January 2026, Makina Finance was exploited for $4.2M through a flash-loan-powered oracle manipulation attack. When we ran the Makina contract through our engine, the core attack path was already flagged — unprotected oracle dependency, permissionless execution of privileged functions, flash loans without invariant enforcement. No new rules added. Score: 100/100. Two cluster multipliers fired. The engine's rule set, sharpened through months of calibration against the exploits in the Time Machine, caught it cold.

This was the moment the engine went from retrospective to predictive. Not by design — by emergence. The same rules trained against years of DeFi exploitation history — and refined by benchmarking against peer-reviewed academic taxonomies (Zhou et al., 2022) — had generalised to detect a threat nobody else caught.
